### [1] [Predictive and feedback signals differently shape the formation of group-level and individualized language representations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.09409)
*Shuguang Yang,Shaoyun Yu,Xin Jiang,Suiping Wang,Gangyi Feng*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: Using fMRI and transformer models, this study found that prediction-based learning signals shape common neural architectures across adult language learners, while feedback-related mechanisms better explain individual differences in learning outcomes.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand what neural signals drive adult language learning processes and individual differences in learning effectiveness.

Method: 102 adults learned an artificial language over 7 days with corrective feedback while undergoing fMRI; matched transformer models with prediction, feedback, or combined objectives were trained and compared to brain activity.

Result: Prediction-focused model accounted for most neural variance at group level despite the task being feedback-based; feedback model was most useful for predicting individual generalization outcomes on Day 7.

Conclusion: A multi-signal model: prediction shapes common neural learning architecture, feedback explains individual differences over time.

Abstract: Adults vary greatly in how effectively they learn a new language, but the signals driving the learning processes and individual differences remain unclear. Over seven days, we tracked behavioral learning and collected fMRI data from 102 adults as they learned an artificial language with corrective feedback. We trained matched transformer models with prediction, feedback, or combined objectives and compared their internal representations to brain activity. Representations derived from the prediction-focused model accounted for the largest share of unique neural variance at the group level, despite the human task being feedback-based. Throughout model training, both objectives showed a shift in brain-model alignment from sensory to higher-order language and associative networks, indicating abstraction processing. Conversely, neural patterns related to the feedback model were most useful for predicting individual generalization outcomes on Day 7. These findings support a multi-signal model of adult language learning, in which prediction shapes a common neural learning architecture across learners, whereas feedback-related mechanisms better explain individual differences over time.

### [2] [Joint sparse coding and temporal dynamics support context reconfiguration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10178)
*Qianqian Shi,Yue Che,Faqiang Liu,Hongyi Li,Mingkun Xu,Sandra Reinert,Pieter M. Goltstein,Rong Zhao,Luping Shi*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper shows that joint sparse coding and temporal dynamics in the mouse mPFC and computational networks help preserve prior representations during context transitions, improving lifelong learning retention without auxiliary heuristics.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how the brain reconfigures neural representations during context transitions without erasing prior knowledge, and to address catastrophic forgetting in artificial lifelong learning systems.

Method: Combined experimental recordings from mouse medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) with computational network modeling, analyzing sparse coding and temporal dynamics during context transitions.

Result: Sparsity reduces cross-context interference; temporal dynamics enhance context separability; spiking neural networks with both properties show improved retention in lifelong learning.

Conclusion: Joint sparse coding and temporal dynamics serve as a core, energy-efficient mechanism supporting flexible context reconfiguration and stable adaptation in lifelong learning.

Abstract: Adaptive behavior requires the brain to transition between distinct contexts while maintaining representations of prior experience. The ability to reconfigure neural representations without erasing previously acquired knowledge is central to learning in dynamic environments, yet the neural mechanisms that support this balance remain unclear. Understanding these mechanisms is also critical for addressing catastrophic forgetting in artificial systems designed for lifelong learning. Here, we identify joint sparse coding and temporal dynamics in both the mouse medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and computational networks as mechanisms that help preserve prior representations during context transitions. Specifically, sparsity in context-dependent representations reduces cross-context interference, whereas temporal dynamics within the network activity further enhance context separability across time. Strikingly, networks endowed with both properties, such as spiking neural networks, exhibit improved retention during lifelong learning without auxiliary heuristics. These findings establish joint sparse coding and temporal dynamics as a core mechanism supporting flexible context reconfiguration in lifelong learning and, through their activity constraining nature, as an energy-efficient architectural principle for stable adaptation. Together, they provide a mechanistic framework for understanding how the brain preserves prior knowledge while flexibly adapting to new contexts.

The cortico-cerebellar RNN (CB-RNN) learns faster and reaches higher maximum performance than parameter-matched fully recurrent baselines across a variety of regimes. Crucially, freezing the recurrent core after minimal training and delegating subsequent learning to the cerebellar module preserves superior learning efficiency, suggesting the cerebellar module is a primary driver of efficiency and that the cortical network can largely function as a fixed reservoir. Our results suggest that heterogeneous modular architectures can act as a powerful structural inductive bias in neural systems.

  Results: The structures presented satisfy symmetry and secondary structure properties which are expected from homology modelling. Over three 500 ns trajectories, there is minor instability of the predicted globular head domain, but the homotrimer otherwise stays mostly folded. The putative NC16A domain is stiff, whereas the truncated Col-15 domain is highly flexible. There does not appear to be a nearby stable conformation distinct from the initial state.
  Conclusion: The structure presented is a useful starting point for targeting BP180 pharmacologically, for further experimental characterization of BP180, and for generating hypotheses regarding the relevant epitopes contributing to bullous disease. Diffusion models such as Boltz-2 and AlphaFold3 are useful, but their results must be evaluated carefully.

To address this gap, we introduce Transition-Directed Discrete Diffusion for Allosteric Binder Design (TD3B), a sequence-based generative framework that designs binders with specified agonist or antagonist behavior via a directional transition control objective. *Özgür Gültekin,Mirza Muradli*

Abstract: Traditional population models that include predator-prey interactions attribute demographic changes directly to predation-related effects. In this study, we propose a cubic population model in which fear acts through two distinct functional channels for a single-species population exhibiting the Allee effect. In this model, fear reduces the intrinsic growth rate through a multiplicative suppression mechanism while also playing an integrated role in modulating the growth and interaction dynamics by rescaling the saturation structure of the Holling type III interaction term. Additionally, statistical properties, parameter sensitivity, and escape dynamics are investigated through normalised moments, Fisher information, and mean first-passage time (MFPT) calculations. Notably, our model treats fear as an independent control parameter and provides a natural explanation for several conflicting empirical findings in the literature on fear-mediated population dynamics, while also offering an analytical basis for conservation biology and ecosystem management.

Main category: q-bio.PE

Method: A minimal model of two competing bacterial species where one species exhibits stochastic and competitor-responsive switching to a persister phenotype. Combined analytical and numerical analysis of traveling wave solutions.

Abstract: Complex microbial habitats see the spatial competition of different clonal bacterial populations that switch between different phenotypes. Here, we determine the effect of this subpopulation structure on the invasion of one species by another in a minimal model of two competing species: one species switches, both stochastically and in response to its competitor, to a persister phenotype resilient to competition. Surprisingly, our combined analytical and numerical results show that this phenotypic switching has no effect on the speed of the travelling wave by which the competitors invade the first population. Conversely, we discover that phenotypic switching can speed up the wave by which this population invades their competitors. Our results thus suggest, counterintuitively, that bacterial persistence can be an offensive, rather than defensive ecological strategy.
