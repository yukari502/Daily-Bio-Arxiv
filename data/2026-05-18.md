### [1] [Beyond Flickering: Introducing Code-Modulated Motion Visual Evoked Potentials for Brain-Computer Interfacing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15801)
*Hanneke Scheppink,Rainer Herpers,Jordy Thielen,Ivan Volosyak*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper proposes a code-modulated motion visual evoked potential (c-MVEP) for BCI, using motion-based pseudo-random sequences instead of flickering stimulation. Offline and online experiments showed c-MVEP achieved 85.67% accuracy (2.61s selection time), outperforming SSMVEP but underperforming c-VEP and SSVEP, while offering a more comfortable motion-based alternative.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To develop a motion-based visual stimulation paradigm (c-MVEP) as an alternative to traditional flickering-based c-VEP for BCI, aiming to reduce visual fatigue while maintaining competitive performance.

Method: The c-MVEP paradigm uses pseudo-random sequences to visually stimulate objects via motion rather than flickering. Both offline (comparing c-MVEP, c-VEP, SSMVEP, SSVEP) and online (4-class BCI) experiments were conducted with EEG recordings.

Result: c-MVEP achieved 85.67% accuracy at 2.61s selection time online. It showed similar time/frequency characteristics to c-VEP with broadband response but lower SNR. It outperformed SSMVEP (64.91%; 4.18s) but underperformed c-VEP (97.81%; 1.15s) and SSVEP (93.42%; 1.94s).

Conclusion: The c-MVEP paradigm shows great potential as a valuable motion-based alternative to flickering-based c-VEP for BCI applications, offering a good balance between performance and user comfort.

Abstract: A code-modulated motion visual evoked potential (c-MVEP) for brain-computer interfacing (BCI) is presented in this study. This paradigm uses pseudo-random sequences to visually stimulate objects using motion as an alternative to flickering. In an offline experiment of this study, EEG data were recorded and compared during sequential stimulation of a single object under four conditions: c-MVEP, code-modulated visual evoked potential (c-VEP), steady-state motion visual evoked potential (SSMVEP), and steady-state visual evoked potential (SSVEP). c-MVEP showed similar time-domain characteristics as c-VEP, and also in the frequency domain c-MVEP evoked a broadband response similar to c-VEP, with a comparable signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), albeit more focused in the lower frequency range. Both SSMVEP and SSVEP showed clear oscillatory responses at the stimulation frequency and harmonics, with a higher SNR for SSVEP than SSMVEP. The spatial distribution of c-MVEP showed the main activation at Oz and spread across multiple electrodes, whereas c-VEP showed less spreading and was more focused at Oz. Similar observations were made for SSMVEP and SSVEP. From subjective ratings, there was no clear preference for the motion-based stimulation of SSMVEP or c-MVEP over flicker-based stimulation of SSVEP or c-VEP. The online experiment of this study, evaluated a 4-class BCI with the same four conditions, testing the practical feasibility of the c-MVEP paradigm. The c-MVEP BCI reached a mean accuracy of 85.67% with an average selection time of 2.61s, which was significantly lower than c-VEP (97.81%; 1.15s) and SSVEP (93.42%; 1.94s), but significantly higher than SSMVEP (64.91%; 4.18s). Overall, this study shows the great potential of the newly proposed c-MVEP paradigm using motion stimulation for BCI applications, providing a valuable alternative to the c-VEP paradigm using flickering stimulation.

### [2] [The Complex Brain Hypothesis: Resolving the Entropy-Content Conundrum in Minimal Phenomenal Experience](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.16146)
*Jonas Mago,Edmundo Lopez-Sola,Jakub Vohryzek,Michael Lifshitz,Robin Carhart-Harris,Karl Friston,Shamil Chandaria*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: The paper introduces the Complex Brain Hypothesis (CBH) to resolve a paradox in the Entropic Brain Hypothesis (EBH): both high-content psychedelic experiences and minimal phenomenal experiences (like deep meditation) show elevated brain entropy despite opposite levels of phenomenal richness. The CBH proposes that brain complexity, not entropy, better indexes phenomenal richness, with different inferential grains producing either content-proliferation or contentless awareness.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The EBH predicts high entropy = high phenomenal richness, but recent neuroimaging shows that MPEs (meditation, possibly 5-MeO-DMT) also exhibit high entropy despite low phenomenal content. This contradiction (the entropy-content conundrum) needs resolution.

Method: The authors propose the Complex Brain Hypothesis (CBH), arguing that complexity (not entropy) distinguishes MPEs from HCPEs. They frame this via inferential grain: fine-grained inference amplifies content (HCPEs), coarse-grained inference dissolves content (MPEs). Both can elevate entropy but differ in complexity and perturbational signatures.

Result: The CBH resolves the conundrum: entropy can be high in both MPEs and HCPEs, but complexity differentiates them. Fine-grained (HCPEs) and coarse-grained (MPEs) inference regimes produce opposite phenomenal outcomes despite similar entropy elevation.

Conclusion: The CBH refines the EBH by identifying complexity as a better marker of phenomenal richness. MPEs are highlighted as critical test cases for computational theories of consciousness, and perturbational methods may empirically distinguish these regimes.

Abstract: Minimal Phenomenal Experiences (MPEs) are states of consciousness in which wakefulness is preserved but phenomenal content is low or absent. The Entropic Brain Hypothesis (EBH) is a model of conscious processes that regards the entropy of spontaneous brain activity as a marker of 'phenomenal richness', exemplified by high-content psychedelic experiences (HCPEs). Yet recent human neuroimaging studies of MPEs induced by meditation -- and possibly 5-MeO-DMT -- suggest that these states, defined by their phenomenological simplicity, also show signs of increased neurophysiological entropy. This presents a conundrum for the EBH: brain entropy is elevated with increased and decreased richness of the phenomenal experience. Here, we put forward the Complex Brain Hypothesis (CBH), which proposes that the richness of experience differentiating MPEs from HCPEs is better indexed by complexity than by entropy. We argue that brain complexity is modulated by the grain of inference through which the brain resolves uncertainty: some HCPEs exemplify a fine-grained regime, in which loosened constraints amplify fluctuations into proliferating content, whereas some MPEs exemplify a coarse-grained regime, in which a simpler model dissolves variety into an experience of 'contentless' awareness. Both regimes can be associated with elevated brain entropy, but they diverge in phenomenology and perturbational signatures. By resolving the entropy-content conundrum, the CBH refines the EBH and highlights MPEs as an important test case for computational theories of consciousness.

### [4] [A geometry-dependent, force balance-driven model of Staphylococcus epidermidis biofilm cell cluster detachment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15364)
*Yuehui Xu,Jasmine A. F. Kreig,Zhuoran Wang,Elizabeth J. Stewart,Rayanne A. Luke,Sarah D. Olson*

Main category: q-bio.QM

TL;DR: This paper presents a new biofilm detachment model that accounts for drag and adhesion at the cluster level, tracking cluster size and morphology rather than simplifying detachment to a function of biofilm thickness. The model is benchmarked against experimental Staphylococcus epidermidis biofilm data and examines how EPS disruption affects cluster frequency, size, and shape.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Existing biofilm detachment models oversimplify the process by making detachment solely a function of biofilm thickness or EPS density, failing to track properties of detached clusters (size, morphology) that affect their biological fate in secondary infections.

Method: A detachment model incorporating drag and adhesion forces in tagged sections of biofilm, determined by cluster geometry and local arrangement of bacteria and EPS. A stickiness parameter controls local EPS adhesion strength, modulated to simulate EPS disruption. The model is benchmarked against experimental microstructural data from 24-hour Staphylococcus epidermidis biofilms.

Result: The model provides quantitative predictions of detached biofilm cell cluster frequency, size, and shape under different EPS disruption levels, offering mechanistic insights into how compromised EPS affects detachment dynamics.

Conclusion: This integrated modeling framework significantly advances predictive capabilities for biofilm detachment processes by connecting microstructural features to cluster-level detachment outcomes.

Abstract: Biofilms, bacteria cells surrounded by a self-produced polymeric matrix, are common on medical devices and lead to many hospital infections. The biofilm lifecycle includes disassembly and dispersion, where bacteria clusters detach from the biofilm, circulate in the bloodstream, and potentially colonize secondary infection sites. Existing models often simplify detachment to a function of biofilm thickness or extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) density, without tracking properties of detached clusters that impact their biological fate, including cluster size and morphology. Addressing this gap, our detachment model accounts for drag and adhesion in tagged sections of the biofilm determined by the cluster geometry and local arrangement of bacteria and EPS. A stickiness parameter controls local EPS adhesion strength, which is modulated to disrupt (or compromise) EPS biomass. We specifically model the detachment of clusters from a Staphylococcus epidermidis biofilm grown for 24 hours. Experimental data for biofilm microstructural features are utilized to benchmark the simulated biofilm, which is then subjected to different EPS disruption levels. We examine parameters that influence detached biofilm cell cluster frequency, size, and shape, providing mechanistic insights into how compromised EPS influences detachment dynamics. This integrated modeling framework is a significant advance in the predictive capabilities for biofilm detachment processes.

Method: LLM-enhanced agent-based model simulating 100 heterogeneous agents (with Heat Vulnerability Index) over 13 days (baseline, heatwave, recovery).

Abstract: Extreme heat events are increasing in frequency and intensity under climate change, but the socio-behavioral mechanisms that shape community resilience remain insufficiently understood. This study uses a Large Language Model-enhanced agent-based model to simulate responses to a prolonged heatwave in a virtual society. ### [6] [How nature discovers rare Turing islands: exploration by common limit cycles](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15839)
*Seyoon Kim,Antonio Matas-Gil,Robert G. Endres*

Main category: q-bio.CB

TL;DR: This paper proposes that biochemical limit cycles (e.g., genetic feedback loops) can help biological systems discover and generate Turing patterns by dynamically sweeping through parameter space, making pattern formation more robust and detectable without requiring precise parameter tuning.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Turing patterns require finely tuned parameters in narrow high-dimensional spaces, posing the question of how evolving biological systems reliably find these rare conditions.

Method: Coupling a reaction-diffusion system to a limit cycle that modulates parameters, using Fourier-space entropy measures to quantify pattern formation, and exploring coupling to positional gradients for reproducibility.

Result: Limit cycles enable transient spatial patterns by dynamically sweeping through Turing-permissive regimes; entropy-based measures confirm enhanced detectability and robustness of Turing islands; positional gradients improve reproducibility.

Conclusion: This mechanism offers a plausible route from oscillatory dynamics to stable developmental programs, suggesting nature can bootstrap complex spatial structure from simple temporal motifs.

Abstract: Turing patterns are a cornerstone of biological self-organization, yet their emergence typically requires finely tuned parameters occupying narrow regions of high-dimensional space. This poses a fundamental challenge: how can evolving biological systems reliably find and exploit such rare conditions? In this work, we propose that common biochemical limit cycles, such as those arising from genetic feedback loops, can act as natural explorers of Turing space. By coupling a reaction-diffusion system to an orbit that modulates some of its parameters, we show that the system can dynamically sweep through Turing-permissive regimes and generate transient spatial patterns. We use an entropy-based measure in Fourier space to quantify pattern formation and demonstrate how cycles enhance the detectability and robustness of Turing islands. We further explore how coupling to positional gradients increases reproducibility, suggesting a route from oscillatory dynamics to stable developmental programs. Our results highlight a powerful mechanism by which nature might bootstrap complex spatial structure from simple temporal motifs.

### [7] [StateXDiff: Cell State-Contextualized Multimodal Diffusion for Single-Cell Perturbation Prediction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.16104)
*Peiting Shi,Ningfeng Que,Xianzhe Huang,Xiaofei Wang,Jianzhong Jeff Xi*

Main category: q-bio.GN

TL;DR: StateXDiff is a multimodal diffusion framework that integrates transcriptomic and protein features with drug knowledge to predict single-cell responses to drug perturbations, achieving superior generalization under unseen cell lines, drugs, and combinatorial perturbations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Predicting drug-induced cellular state changes at single-cell resolution under out-of-distribution conditions is challenging due to RNA-based assays failing to capture diverse cellular states, conditional distribution shifts, and low signal-to-noise ratios causing spurious correlations.

Method: StateXDiff learns a disentangled multimodal representation (transcriptomic + inferred protein features), then uses a conditional diffusion model with a latent-space diffusion Transformer, quality-aware triplet constraints, and a Mechanism-aware Drug-Gene Template to generate perturbation-specific changes.

Result: StateXDiff consistently enhances generalization across unseen cell lines, unseen drugs, and combinatorial perturbations.

Conclusion: Integrating multimodal cellular state representations with mechanism-aware drug templates and diffusion-based generation improves OOD generalization for single-cell drug response prediction.

Abstract: Predicting drug-induced cellular state changes at single-cell resolution remains a central challenge in virtual cell modeling, particularly under out-of-distribution (OOD) conditions. Current approaches predominantly rely on RNA-based assays, which often fail to adequately capture the diverse cellular states underlying drug responses. Moreover, conditional distribution shifts and low signal-to-noise ratios frequently cause models to learn spurious correlations rather than genuine state transitions. To address these limitations, we introduce StateXDiff, a cell State-contextualized multimodal (X) Diffusion framework for predicting single-cell responses to drug perturbations. The framework operates sequentially: first, it learns a disentangled, multimodal representation of cellular state by integrating transcriptomic profiles with inferred protein features; second, it employs a conditional diffusion model to generate perturbation-specific changes. Our approach introduces a Virtual Multimodal Cell State, which augments RNA-based representations with protein-level context, and a Mechanism-aware Drug-Gene Template, which consolidates multi-source biological knowledge for accurate drug representation. Generation is driven by a latent-space diffusion Transformer, regularized through quality-aware triplet constraints, including positive drug-protein pairs or protein-drug mismatched pairs, and explicit protein-reliability weighting. Extensive evaluation demonstrates that StateXDiff consistently enhances generalization performance across three challenging settings: unseen cell lines, unseen drugs, and combinatorial perturbations.

</details>
