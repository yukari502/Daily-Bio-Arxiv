### [1] [SpCAST: Decoding spatial transcriptomics at single-cell resolution with fast and interpretable analysis](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26904)
*Yiyang Zhang,Bokai Zhao,Xiaoru Zhang,Zongchang Du,Xiaojuan Sun,Tianzi Jiang*

Main category: q-bio.CB

TL;DR: SpCAST is a Kolmogorov-Arnold network-based framework for reference-guided spatial transcriptomics analysis that performs cell-type label transfer, spatial gene-expression reconstruction, and marker-gene prioritization, achieving competitive performance on 53 datasets across five technologies.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Accurate cell-type annotation in spatial transcriptomics remains challenging due to limited gene panels in imaging-based platforms and sparse molecular capture in sequencing-based platforms, necessitating reliable label transfer from single-cell RNA sequencing references.

Method: SpCAST uses a Kolmogorov-Arnold network to capture nonlinear mappings between reference and spatial expression profiles, with feature attribution to prioritize genes supporting cell-type predictions.

Result: Benchmarked on 53 datasets with 413,376 spatial cells across five technologies, SpCAST achieved competitive annotation performance with reduced runtime. It supports cross-species label transfer, candidate assignment of unlabeled cells, and reconstructs marker-gene expression with improved spatial concordance.

Conclusion: SpCAST is an efficient and interpretable framework for extracting cell-type and gene-level information from targeted and sparse single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics data.

Abstract: Single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics profiles gene expression at cellular locations in native tissues, yet accurate cell-type annotation remains challenging: imaging-based platforms are constrained by targeted gene panels, whereas sequencing-based platforms often suffer from sparse molecular capture and dropout. Reliable transfer of cell-type labels from single-cell RNA sequencing references is therefore critical for interpreting targeted and sparse spatial datasets. Here, we present SpCAST, a Kolmogorov--Arnold network-based framework for reference-guided spatial transcriptomics analysis. SpCAST captures nonlinear mappings between reference and spatial expression profiles and uses feature attribution to prioritize genes supporting cell-type predictions. Within a unified framework, SpCAST performs cell-type label transfer, spatial gene-expression reconstruction and marker-gene candidate prioritization. We benchmarked SpCAST on 53 datasets comprising 413,376 spatial cells across five technologies and diverse tissue contexts. SpCAST achieved competitive annotation performance with reduced runtime relative to representative existing methods. Case studies demonstrated that SpCAST supports cross-species label transfer and candidate assignment of originally unlabeled cells. It also reconstructs marker-gene expression patterns with improved spatial concordance and prioritizes cell-type-associated marker genes. Together, these results support SpCAST as an efficient and interpretable framework for extracting cell-type and gene-level information from targeted and sparse single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics data.

### [2] [Random neural networks match observed dimensionality of neural population recordings and motivate stronger experimental tests](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26551)
*Zehui Zhao,Michael J Pasek,Ilya M Nemenman*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: The paper tests whether random neural network models can quantitatively match experimentally measured neural population activity, finding that after accounting for finite recording time and behavioral variability, random models can explain observed low-dimensional activity, but current data durations limit distinguishability of connectivity structures.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To determine whether minimally structured random neural networks can quantitatively account for the low-dimensional structure observed in large-scale neural population recordings, bridging a gap between theoretical models and experimental data.

Method: Building on Dynamical Mean-Field Theory, the authors incorporate finite measurement time and cross-context variability into random neural network models, then compare predicted dimensionality and manifold orientation similarity to experimental recordings.

Result: Measured dimensionality from large-scale recordings is consistent with random model predictions when finite measurement time and behavioral variability are included; dimensionality varies non-monotonically with input strength; orientation similarity of neural manifolds across contexts is more sensitive to connectivity structure than dimensionality is.

Conclusion: Random models can explain observed population activity patterns given current experimental limitations, but distinguishing connectivity structures requires careful experimental design—orientation similarity across contexts may serve as a more informative metric than dimensionality alone.

Abstract: Randomly connected neural networks have long served as a theoretical tool for studying collective dynamics in neural populations, yet quantitative comparisons to experiments remain limited. Recent technological advances have made it possible to resolve population-wide correlations across neurons, and minimal models such as random neural networks predict their generic structure. Whether the two agree quantitatively remains untested. In this work, we examine whether a minimally structured random neural network can account for the low dimensionality of activity in neural population recordings by building on recent developments in Dynamical Mean-Field Theory and incorporating two additional experimentally relevant features into the model: finite measurement time and variability across behavioral contexts. We show that, when these factors are included, the dimensionality measured from large-scale recordings is consistent with the values predicted by random models. However, current recording durations make it difficult to use dimensionality to discriminate among connectivity structures. We further show that analytically predicted dimensionality varies non-monotonically with external input strength, and that the orientation similarity between neural manifolds recorded under different behavioral contexts can be more sensitive to network structure than dimensionality is. Together, these results provide quantitative guidance for experimental design to infer the connectivity structure underlying population activity.

TL;DR: This paper proposes the Sensation Modulating Network (SMN) as a unified architecture for embodied cognition that reconciles cognitivism and 4E approaches. The SMN is built from opponent dynamics across anatomical scales, with key commitments including haltability (enabling attention/intentionality), dual-signal self-modulatable action patterns (distinguishing self/world structurally), and a four-level action hierarchy (from autonomic to conventional grammar-grounded generativity).


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To resolve the impasse between cognitivism (which handles recursion/language but cannot ground symbols) and 4E approaches (which ground cognition in the body but lack architectural specificity for generativity).

Method: Proposes the Sensation Modulating Network (SMN) - a whole-body cognitive architecture organized by opponent dynamics, composed of Sensation Modulators paired into Coordinated Action Zones via body-wide broadcast. Three commitments: (1) Haltability via opponent co-activation enabling intentionality; (2) Dual-signal SMAPs making self/world distinction structural; (3) Four-level action hierarchy (Basal→Haltable→Negotiable→Transactional).

Result: A framework that reconciles cognitivism and 4E approaches: recursion emerges from modifiable dynamics of Negotiable Action Patterns, embodiment from the opponent substrate. Includes a formal appendix with 8 predicted registers (7 testable, 1 hypothetical) and reference simulations.

Abstract: Cognitive science remains split between cognitivism - which accounts for recursion and language but cannot ground formal symbols in meaning - and 4E approaches - which ground cognition in the body but rarely specify the body's architecture in enough detail to support generativity. We argue the impasse stems from an incomplete account of the embodied agent's architecture, and propose one: the Sensation Modulating Network (SMN), the cognitive agent conceived as the whole body, organized at every anatomical scale by opponent dynamics, built from Sensation Modulators that sense and act through one substrate, paired into Coordinated Action Zones routed by a body-wide broadcast network. Three commitments give the SMN its purchase. Haltability - the recruitment of antagonistic affordance into co-activated equilibrium - provides the architectural locus that object-directed phenomenology, in Husserl's sense, requires: opponency enables co-activation, co-activation enables halt, halt enables attention, attention enables intentional directedness, with no module added on top. The dual-signal property of self-modulatable action patterns (SMAPs) makes the self/world distinction a structural feature of the wiring rather than a category the agent applies. And a four-level action-pattern hierarchy - Basal, Haltable, Negotiable, Transactional - gives a single trajectory from autonomic regularity to public conventionalization, locating the conditions for grammar-grounded generativity as architectural transitions. The SMN reconciles the cognitivism-4E debate: recursion lives in the modifiable dynamics of Negotiable Action Patterns, embodiment in the opponent substrate that supports them. A tentative formalism and eight predicted registers (seven testable, one hypothetical), with reference simulations, are given in an appendix.

### [4] [Fixation location in structured populations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26411)
*David A. Brewster,Gabor Lippner,Josef Tkadlec,Martin A. Nowak*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper introduces "fixation location" as a new concept in evolutionary dynamics on graphs—the node where the last wild-type individual resides before mutant fixation. The distribution is often highly nonuniform, depends on graph structure and selection strength, and some nodes can never be fixation locations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Classical fixation studies (probability and time) ignore spatial aspects of extinction. The authors aim to characterize where a lineage disappears in structured populations, which is relevant for monitoring and mitigating extinctions.

Method: The authors define fixation location as the node occupied by the last wild-type individual immediately before mutant fixation. They analyze this distribution analytically and computationally for neutral evolution, colonization, and constant selection on small graphs, cycles, tori, random graphs, and island populations.

Result: Fixation location distribution is often highly nonuniform, strongly depends on graph structure and selection strength, and can differ sharply even when classical fixation statistics (probability/time) are similar. Some nodes can never be fixation locations on certain graphs.

Conclusion: Fixation location is a fundamental aspect of evolutionary dynamics, offering new insights for understanding, monitoring, and potentially mitigating extinction events in biological and social systems.

Abstract: In stochastic evolutionary dynamics, the replacement of an existing genotype or cultural trait by a newly introduced mutant is typically characterized by the quantities of fixation probability and fixation time. But in a structured population, the disappearance of a lineage occurs at a specific place. For evolutionary dynamics on graphs, we define the fixation location as the node occupied by the last wild-type individual immediately before mutant fixation. Conditional on fixation, this location is described by a probability distribution over the nodes of the graph. We study the fixation location for neutral evolution, for the colonization process, and, more generally, for constant selection on small graphs, cycles, tori, random graphs, and island populations. We find that the distribution of the fixation location is often highly nonuniform, depends strongly on the graph structure and the selection strength, and can differ sharply even when classical fixation statistics are similar. For many graphs, some nodes can never be fixation locations. Our results identify fixation location as a fundamental aspect of evolutionary dynamics and suggest new ways to understand, monitor, and potentially mitigate extinction events in biological and social settings.

</details>


### [5] [Recognizing Level-k-Based Phylogenetic Networks is NP-Complete](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26852)
*Takatora Suzuki*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper proves that determining the minimum support network level of a rooted almost-binary phylogenetic network is NP-complete, confirming Suzuki and Hayamizu's conjecture.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To quantify how far phylogenetic networks are from being tree-based, as not all networks are tree-based.

Method: NP-completeness proof showing that for every fixed integer k≥1, deciding whether the minimum level is at most k is NP-complete.

Result: The conjecture by Suzuki and Hayamizu (2025) that the problem is NP-hard is proven correct.

Conclusion: The minimum support network level problem is computationally intractable (NP-complete).

Abstract: Phylogenetic networks generalize phylogenetic trees by representing reticulate evolution. Tree-based networks and their support trees have been extensively studied, but not all networks are tree-based. To measure how far such networks are from being tree-based, Suzuki and Hayamizu (2025) formulated the problem of finding the support network with minimum level of a given rooted almost-binary phylogenetic network. They conjectured that this problem is NP-hard and provided exponential-time algorithms. In this paper, we prove this conjecture by showing that, for every fixed integer $k \geq 1$, it is NP-complete to decide whether the minimum level is at most $k$.

### [6] [Logistic dynamics of small populations with demographic stochasticity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27100)
*Lucas M. Brugevin,Damián H. Zanette*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper studies a stochastic ecology-inspired population model where birth, death, and immigration events create alternating active and extinct periods. Unlike deterministic logistic models, active periods show bimodality (short/small or long/large populations). Network structures (Erdős-Rényi, regular, geographic) cause population clustering, limiting growth and increasing extinction frequency.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how stochastic fluctuations in finite populations deviate from deterministic mean-field predictions, and to characterize the statistics of alternating active/extinct periods under different network structures.

Method: Analytical and numerical techniques applied to a stochastic birth-death-immigration model on bounded populations, studied on three random network topologies: Erdős-Rényi, regular, and geographic networks.

Result: Active periods exhibit pronounced bimodality (short/small vs. long/large populations), contrasting with deterministic logistic dynamics. Network structure induces population clustering, which limits growth and increases vacant period frequency.

Conclusion: Stochastic effects and network topology fundamentally alter population dynamics from mean-field expectations, with clustering and bimodality being key emergent features.

Abstract: We study an ecology-inspired model for a population of bounded size, whose dynamics is governed by random birth, death, and immigration events. Stochastic fluctuations in the number of individuals give rise to a succession of alternating active and vacant periods, where the population is respectively extant and extinct. Using both analytical and numerical techniques, we characterize the statistics of the two kinds of period, quantifying their duration and frequency, and the typical population sizes in active periods. In sharp contrast to the deterministic mean-field behavior, governed by logistic dynamics, active periods may exhibit pronounced bimodality: either short durations with very small populations, or much longer durations with population sizes close to the maximum. We also investigate how these results change when the population evolves on random networks of three classes: Erdős-Rényi, regular, and geographic. The main effect of the network structure is to induce population clustering, with individuals aggregated into localized groups. This, in turn, limits population growth and increases the frequency of vacant periods.

### [7] [What Molecular Structure Cannot Tell Us: A Taxonomy of Explainability Gaps in GNN-Based Drug Toxicity Prediction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26183)
*Juergen Dietrich*

Main category: q-bio.QM

TL;DR: This paper uses Aspirin as a case study to show that molecular structure only explains ~45% of known adverse effects, introducing a four-category Gap Taxonomy (GAP 1-4) to classify why toxicity information cannot be encoded solely from structure, with implications for drug safety and regulatory frameworks.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To systematically investigate what fraction of a drug's known pharmacological profile is actually encodable in its molecular structure for GNN-based toxicity prediction, a question that remains underexplored.

Method: Uses a Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN) trained on Tox21, applies GNNExplainer for atom-level attribution, introduces a Gap Taxonomy (GAP-1 through GAP-4), conducts a systematic ChEMBL query (42 assays, 0 retrievable entries), and performs attention pooling experiments to localize representation errors.

Result: Molecular structure explains ~45% (5/11) of known ASA adverse effects. The MNAR gap is empirically quantified (42 documented assays, 0 retrievable bioactivity entries). Representation error is localized to MPNN message passing layers rather than the aggregation step.

Conclusion: The Gap Taxonomy has direct implications for drug safety signal detection and regulatory frameworks including GVP guidelines and NAMs, highlighting fundamental limitations of structure-only toxicity prediction.

Abstract: Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a structurally natural approach for molecular toxicity prediction, operating directly on atomic connectivity without the information loss inherent to fixed-length fingerprints. However, the fraction of a drug's known pharmacological profile that is actually encodable in its molecular structure remains systematically underexplored. This study addresses this question through a systematic case study using acetylsalicylic acid (ASA, Aspirin) - one of the most comprehensively characterized drugs in pharmacology - as a model compound. A Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN) is trained on the Tox21 benchmark and GNNExplainer is applied to characterize atom-level attribution. Results indicate that molecular structure explains approximately 45% (5/11) of known ASA adverse effects. A four-category Gap Taxonomy (GAP-1 through GAP-4) is introduced distinguishing between principally non-encodable effects, data gaps arising from Missing Not At Random (MNAR) mechanisms, assay panel mismatches, and representation errors. The MNAR gap is empirically quantified via a systematic ChEMBL query (42 documented assays, 0 retrievable bioactivity entries). An attention pooling experiment localizes the representation error to the MPNN message passing layers rather than the aggregation step. The Gap Taxonomy has direct implications for drug safety signal detection workflows and regulatory frameworks including Good Pharmacovigilance Practice (GVP) guidelines and New Approach Methodologies (NAMs).

</details>
