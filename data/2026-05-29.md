### [1] [Phylogenetic dynamics of MRCA ages and empirical moments of a Brownian trait](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29736)
*Gilles Didier*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper analyzes the temporal dynamics of empirical mean and variance of Brownian traits on phylogenetic trees, showing piecewise linear behavior and connecting these quantities to the expected MRCA age of extant lineages, with explicit formulas for birth-death processes.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how the first two empirical moments (mean and variance) of continuously evolving traits change over time on phylogenetic trees, and to characterize their distributional properties under different tree generation models.

Method: The authors analytically characterize distributions of empirical mean and variance on fixed trees, derive relationships with MRCA age for random trees, and obtain explicit formulas for generalized birth-death processes with time-dependent rates, including closed-form solutions for the constant-rate case.

Result: Key findings: (1) Variance of empirical mean and expected empirical variance are piecewise linear between diversification events on fixed trees; (2) For random trees, these quantities are expressed via expected MRCA age with opposite signs; (3) Explicit formulas derived for birth-death processes with asymptotic behavior characterized in supercritical, critical, and subcritical regimes.

Conclusion: The expected MRCA age serves as a unifying quantity linking the variance of empirical mean and expected empirical variance in opposition. The results provide a mathematical framework for understanding trait moment dynamics on phylogenies, with closed-form expressions available under constant-rate birth-death models.

Abstract: We study the temporal dynamics of the first two empirical moments of Brownian traits on phylogenetic trees. For a fixed tree, we characterize the distributions of their empirical mean and empirical variance across all lineages extant at any given time. In particular, we show that the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance are piecewise linear between diversification events.
  For lineage-homogeneous random trees, both the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance can be expressed in terms of the expected age of the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) of a uniformly sampled pair of extant lineages. In this representation, the expected MRCA age enters the two quantities with opposite signs, pointing to a structural opposition between the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance.
  For generalized birth-death processes with time-dependent speciation and extinction rates, we derive an explicit formula for the distribution of the MRCA age of a uniformly sampled pair of extant lineages. This yields integral expressions, at any time, for both the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance. In the constant-rate birth-death case, we further obtain closed-form expressions for the expected empirical variance and describe its asymptotic behavior in the supercritical, critical and subcritical regimes.

### [2] [Lattice Brownian bees with cooperative reproduction: steady states, collapse, and spreading](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29958)
*Ohad Vilk,Baruch Meerson*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper extends the Brownian bees model to cooperative reproduction ($kA \to (k+1)A$) with far-particle removal, deriving a hydrodynamic free-boundary problem. It finds steady states for all $k$, proves linear stability for $k\le 2$ and instability for $k\ge 4$, with $k=3$ as a marginal case exhibiting a continuous family of steady states. Above criticality the population collapses in finite time; below it spreads diffusively.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how cooperative reproduction (multiple particles cooperating to produce offspring) affects the spatial structure and critical behavior of a Brownian branching-and-removal system, generalizing prior work on the $k=1$ (pairwise branching) Brownian bees model.

Method: Hydrodynamic free-boundary problem formulation in the $N\to\infty$ limit, linear stability analysis, matched-asymptotic expansion for collapse dynamics, validated by numerical PDE solutions and Monte Carlo simulations of the microscopic model.

Result: Steady state densities derived for all $k$; linear stability for $k\le 2$, instability for $k\ge 4$; $k=3$ is marginal with a continuous family of steady states at a critical ratio. Above criticality: finite-time self-similar collapse; below criticality: diffusive spreading with quantitative reproduction effects. For $k\ge 4$, the unstable steady state separates collapse and spreading regimes.

Conclusion: Cooperative reproduction introduces richer phase behavior and critical transitions (marginal $k=3$, unstable $k\ge 4$) not present in the original pairwise branching model, with analytical predictions confirmed by numerics and simulations.

Abstract: We extend the ``Brownian bees'' model of Berestycki et al. (2021, 2022) to cooperative reproduction, $kA\to(k{+}1)A$, of a population of $N$ symmetric random walkers with removal, at each birth event, of the particle farthest from the origin. Working in the limit $N\to\infty$, we formulate a hydrodynamic free-boundary problem for this model. Using this formalism, we determine steady state population densities for all~$k$ and prove their linear stability for $k\le 2$ and instability for $k\ge 4$. In the marginal case $k=3$, there is a whole continuous family of steady states at a single, critical ratio of the reproduction and diffusion rates. Above criticality the population undergoes an asymptotically self-similar finite-time collapse to the origin. Below the criticality the population spreads diffusively, but the reproduction remains quantitatively relevant. For $k\ge 4$, the unstable steady state separates regimes of a finite-time collapse and a diffusive spreading. Here the collapse dynamics is asymptotically self-similar, and the population density exhibits a scale separation requiring a matched-asymptotic description. Our analytical predictions are confirmed by numerical solutions of the hydrodynamic free-boundary problem and by Monte Carlo simulations of the original microscopic model.

### [3] [Training Ecosystems: A Computational Approach to Uncovering Learning Behavior in Unconventional Contexts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30109)
*Adrita Samanta,Hananel Hazan,Michael Levin*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper shows that simple predator-prey ecological dynamics can produce learning behaviors (habituation, sensitization, and number learning) without any memory substrate, purely through dynamical interactions, with learning capacity primarily determined by ecological interaction strengths.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: There are knowledge gaps about learning capacity above the organism level (ecological/collective systems) and about memory implemented purely by dynamical interactions without explicit memory media.

Method: Systematic exploration of over 220,000 parameter combinations in a simulated classic predator-prey model, perturbed by stimuli to measure changes in response magnitude and recovery time, with robustness analysis and dimensionality reduction.

Result: Recovery time exhibits habituation, sensitization, and scale-invariant discrete number learning. Habituation/sensitization persist under noise; number learning is disrupted by noise. 90.6% of parameter combinations show recovery time sensitization paired with habituation of response magnitude. Learning capacity is determined by ecological interaction strengths.

Conclusion: Learning capacity arises from mathematics itself, not dependent on specific physical or biological details, with implications for ecology, basal cognition, and engineering systems describable by similar equations.

Abstract: Recent progress in diverse intelligence has shown simple learning capacities below the organism level - single cells and even molecular networks. However, there are still many knowledge gaps around learning capacity above the organism level, and about memory implemented purely by dynamical interactions without explicit memory media. We demonstrate that minimal ecological dynamics (in silico) are sufficient for several kinds of learning, assayed as changes in both, magnitude of response, and of recovery time. Systematic exploration of over 220,000 parameter combinations in a simulated classic predator-prey model revealed that, when perturbed by stimuli, recovery time exhibits habituation, sensitization, and a form of discrete number learning in a scale-invariant manner. Robustness analysis revealed that habituation and sensitization persist under stochastic perturbations, while discrete number learning is disrupted even at low noise levels. Dimensionality reduction revealed that the incidence of learning capacity is primarily determined by ecological interaction strengths. Clear, unique clustering patterns in parameter space allow high prediction accuracy for novel parameter combinations that enable learning. Response magnitude revealed a striking asymmetry: 90.6% of parameter combinations exhibited recovery time sensitization paired with habituation of response magnitude, while the opposite pattern was extremely rare. These findings highlight a set of phenomena at the intersection of ecology, basal cognition, and mathematics with many implications for a wide range of systems describable by similar kinds of equations. These properties provide numerous efforts in biology and engineering with a substrate that has considerable, pre-patterned, propensity for learning, which ultimately arises from mathematics, not depending on the details of physics or biology.

### [4] [Mixing Vector Model for Copolymer Inference via Mixed Integer Linear Programming](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29329)
*Jianshen Zhu,Raveena Rai,Taiyo Sohkawa,Naveed Ahmed Azam,Kazuya Haraguchi,Liang Zhao,Tatsuya Akutsu*

Main category: q-bio.QM

TL;DR: This paper extends the mol-infer framework to copolymers using a mixing vector (MV) model, enabling MILP-based inverse design with good predictive performance across multiple datasets.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To extend the existing two-phase molecule inference framework (mol-infer) from small molecules to copolymers, enabling exact inverse design of copolymer structures with desired properties.

Method: Introduces the mixing vector (MV) model, where copolymer feature vectors are convex combinations of monomer descriptors weighted by mixing ratios. Uses ANN, reduced quadratic multiple linear regression, and random forests for prediction. Formulates multi-monomer inverse-design as MILP.

Result: Achieves test R² > 0.7 for 9/10 datasets and > 0.9 for 6 datasets. The MILP instances remain tractable even for three-monomer settings. External consistency check validates the framework.

Conclusion: The proposed framework provides a tractable first step toward model-level exact inverse design of copolymers under the two-layered model.

Abstract: A novel two-phase molecule inference framework, mol-infer, has recently been developed to infer chemical graphs with prescribed abstract structures and desired property values through mixed integer linear programming (MILP) under the two-layered model, with guaranteed optimality and exactness relative to the given learned prediction function and structural constraints. In this study, we extend this framework to copolymers by introducing a simple feature representation, called the mixing vector (MV) model. In the proposed model, a copolymer feature vector is represented as a convex combination of MILP-tractable monomer descriptors weighted by the mixing ratio of the constituent monomers. This representation does not require explicit sequence-class information and is therefore naturally compatible with MILP-based inverse design. Under this model, we construct prediction functions for several copolymer property datasets using artificial neural networks, reduced quadratic multiple linear regression, and random forests. The proposed representation achieves practically useful predictive performance across multiple physicochemical property datasets; in particular, the best test R^2 score exceeds 0.7 for nine of the ten datasets and exceeds 0.9 for six datasets. We also formulate a multi-monomer inverse-design problem under the MV representation with a prescribed mixing ratio and show that the resulting MILP instances remain tractable, even for three-monomer settings. Finally, we perform an external consistency check by re-evaluating the inferred candidates and comparing the re-computed property values with those predicted by the learned model. Overall, the proposed framework gives a tractable first step toward model-level exact inverse design of copolymers under the two-layered model.

### [5] [FPLIER: Federated Pathway-Level Information Extractor](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29587)
*Daniele Malpetti,Christian Berchtold,Francesco Gualdi,Marco Scutari,Laura Azzimonti,Francesca Mangili*

Main category: q-bio.QM

TL;DR: FPLIER extends PLIER to a federated setting, enabling privacy-preserving, algebraically equivalent distributed transcriptomics training across multiple data holders while demonstrating that membership inference risk drops to random guessing when data dimensionality is reduced or public data is incorporated.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: PLIER is effective for gene-set-aware factorization on large expression compendia, but many clinically relevant datasets cannot be pooled due to privacy and governance constraints. FPLIER addresses this by enabling distributed training across data holders.

Method: FPLIER is a federated extension of PLIER using secure aggregation to produce training updates algebraically equivalent to centralized pooled training, while keeping expression data local.

Result: FPLIER achieves stable convergence across simulated consortia (K-CLIER, MultiPLIER). Privacy risk is governed by the rank of the training expression matrix; incorporating public data or reducing dimensionality increases rank, making membership inference attacks perform near random guessing.

Conclusion: FPLIER provides a privacy-preserving federated alternative to centralized PLIER training, with analytical guarantees on membership inference risk that approach random guessing under full-rank conditions.

Abstract: In transcriptomics, gene-set-aware factorization methods such as the Pathway Level Information Extractor (PLIER) are most effective when trained on large, heterogeneous expression compendia. Yet, many clinically relevant cohorts cannot be pooled into a single dataset due to privacy and governance constraints. We present FPLIER, a federated extension of PLIER that enables distributed training across multiple data holders while incorporating publicly available datasets. Through secure aggregation, FPLIER produces training updates algebraically equivalent to those of a centralized pooled-data approach while keeping expression data local. We evaluate FPLIER across multiple scenarios in two simulated consortia (from the K-CLIER and MultiPLIER studies) and demonstrate stable convergence. We further conduct a systematic analysis of membership inference attacks targeting both intermediate training statistics and the released model. Our results show that privacy risk is governed by the rank of the training expression matrix. Incorporating public data or reducing data dimensionality increases this rank, moving the system toward a full-rank regime in which training and non-training samples become indistinguishable to the attacker, and membership-inference performance approaches random guessing.

### [6] [Stochastic network epidemic model and particle filter: General framework and application to influenza in Japan](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29907)
*Ihtisham Ul Haq,Serge Richard*

Main category: q-bio.QM

TL;DR: A particle filter framework is developed for estimating states and parameters in stochastic epidemic spread on 2D lattice graphs, using two methods (infection count-based and spatial location-based), validated on synthetic data and applied to Japanese influenza data (2024-2025) with one-week-ahead forecasting.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Parameter inference and state estimation in stochastic partially observed biological systems (especially graph-based epidemic models) remain challenging due to randomness and incomplete observations.

Method: A particle filter based data assimilation framework on a two-dimensional lattice graph model, with two methodologies: one using the number of infected agents, another using partial spatial location information of infected agents.

Result: Both methods were validated using synthetic data; the first method was applied to real influenza data from Japanese prefectures (July 2024 - December 2025), with one-week-ahead forecasting performed using current weekly data.

Conclusion: The proposed PF framework is effective for real-time epidemic monitoring, forecasting, and adaptive public health decision-making.

Abstract: Parameter inference and state estimation in stochastic and partially observed biological systems remain major problems in mathematical biology. In this work, we introduce a two-dimensional lattice graph model for the spread of infectious diseases. Estimating states and parameters in graph-based stochastic epidemic systems is particularly challenging because of randomness and incomplete observations. To address these issues, we propose a particle filter based data assimilation framework for the sequential estimation of both model states and unknown parameters. Two methodologies are developed: one based on the number of infected agents and another based on partial spatial location's information of infected agents on a two-dimensional lattice. The performance of the two methods are firstly analyzed and validated using synthetic data, and the first method is then applied to influenza data collected from different prefectures in Japan between July 2024 and December 2025. One-week-ahead forecasting simulations are also performed using current weekly data. The findings highlight the effectiveness of the proposed PF framework for real-time epidemic monitoring, forecasting, and adaptive public health decision-making.
