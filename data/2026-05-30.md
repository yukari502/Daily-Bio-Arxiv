### [1] [On a phenotype-structured Shigesada--Kawasaki--Teramoto model: Turing instability and pattern selection under fast phenotype switching](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28976)
*Davide Cusseddu,Gaetana Gambino,Tommaso Lorenzi*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: The paper generalizes the classical SKT model to account for phenotypic variability in competing populations, showing how fast phenotype switching recovers the classical SKT model and how phenotype distributions influence Turing pattern formation.

  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The classical SKT model assumes phenotypic homogeneity, but real populations exhibit phenotypic variability that strongly influences ecological and evolutionary dynamics. The authors aim to bridge this gap by developing a phenotype-structured formulation.

Method: The authors develop a generalized phenotype-structured SKT model with continuous phenotype spaces, analyze it in the quasi-invariant regime of fast phenotype switching, and extend linear and weakly nonlinear Turing analyses to study pattern formation.

Result: The classical SKT model emerges from the generalized framework under fast phenotype switching. Phenotype distributions act as effective control parameters for Turing instability and pattern selection. Numerical simulations validate the analytical predictions.

Conclusion: Phenotype variability can be systematically incorporated into cross-diffusion models, and phenotype distributions play a key role in determining spatial pattern formation in competing populations.

Abstract: The Shigesada-Kawasaki-Teramoto (SKT) model has become a classical modelling framework for studying spatial segregation and cross-diffusion-driven pattern formation in competing populations. This model assumes phenotypic homogeneity, but phenotypic variability persists within any population and can strongly influence both ecological and evolutionary dynamics. In this paper, we present a generalised phenotype-structured formulation of the SKT model that accounts for phenotypic variability. In this formulation, the competing populations are continuously structured across some phenotype state spaces. Population members move and compete in phenotype-dependent ways, and can also switch between different phenotype states. First we show how a form of the classical SKT model, wherein parameters are written in terms of continuous weighted averages of the phenotype-dependent functions of the generalised structured model, with weights given by the phenotype distributions of the two populations, can be obtained in the quasi-invariant regime of fast phenotype switching. Then, still assuming fast phenotype switching and extending classical Turing-like linear and weakly nonlinear analyses, we explore the conditions for the emergence of spatial patterns, identify a Turing-type bifurcation threshold leading to pattern formation, and investigate the nature of such a bifurcation (super- or sub-critical) as well as the stability of the patterned state. The results obtained make it possible to draw connections between phenotype-dependent model functions and the emergence of population-scale aggregate spatial dynamics, showing in particular how phenotype distributions can act as effective control parameters for Turing instability and pattern selection. These findings are complemented by numerical simulations, which validate the formal asymptotics and confirm the predictions of the pattern formation analyses.

TL;DR: This paper characterizes the temporal dynamics of empirical moments (mean and variance) of Brownian traits on phylogenetic trees. <details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how Brownian trait moments evolve over time on phylogenetic trees, particularly how empirical mean and variance behave across lineages as a function of tree structure and diversification dynamics.

Method: Theoretical analysis combining phylogenetic tree dynamics with Brownian motion trait evolution. Uses distributional characterization for fixed trees, expected MRCA age representation for random trees, and explicit formulas for generalized birth-death processes with time-dependent rates.

Result: (1) Variance of empirical mean and expected empirical variance are piecewise linear between diversification events. (2) Both quantities expressible via expected MRCA age with opposite signs. (3) Explicit formulas for MRCA age distribution under generalized birth-death processes. (4) Closed-form expressions for constant-rate birth-death case across supercritical, critical, and subcritical regimes.

Conclusion: The paper establishes a structural opposition between the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance, mediated by MRCA age, and provides tractable analytical results for both fixed and random trees under various diversification scenarios.

Abstract: We study the temporal dynamics of the first two empirical moments of Brownian traits on phylogenetic trees. For a fixed tree, we characterize the distributions of their empirical mean and empirical variance across all lineages extant at any given time. In particular, we show that the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance are piecewise linear between diversification events.
  For lineage-homogeneous random trees, both the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance can be expressed in terms of the expected age of the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) of a uniformly sampled pair of extant lineages. In this representation, the expected MRCA age enters the two quantities with opposite signs, pointing to a structural opposition between the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance.
  For generalized birth-death processes with time-dependent speciation and extinction rates, we derive an explicit formula for the distribution of the MRCA age of a uniformly sampled pair of extant lineages. This yields integral expressions, at any time, for both the variance of the empirical mean and the expected empirical variance. In the constant-rate birth-death case, we further obtain closed-form expressions for the expected empirical variance and describe its asymptotic behavior in the supercritical, critical and subcritical regimes.

### [3] [Lattice Brownian bees with cooperative reproduction: steady states, collapse, and spreading](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29958)
*Ohad Vilk,Baruch Meerson*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper extends the Brownian bees model to cooperative reproduction (kA → (k+1)A) in a population of N symmetric random walkers, analyzing steady states, stability transitions, and collapse/spreading dynamics via hydrodynamic free-boundary problems, with key findings for different k values including a critical case at k=3 and instability for k≥4.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how cooperative reproduction (requiring multiple particles for a birth event) affects the spatial and temporal dynamics of a population under a removal-at-birth constraint, extending prior work on the Brownian bees model which used simple binary reproduction.

Method: The authors formulate a hydrodynamic free-boundary problem in the N→∞ limit, analyze steady-state population densities, perform linear stability analysis, and validate analytical results with numerical solutions and Monte Carlo simulations.

Result: Steady states exist for all k, with linear stability for k≤2, instability for k≥4, and a marginal continuous family of steady states at k=3 at a critical ratio. Above criticality: finite-time self-similar collapse; below: diffusive spreading. For k≥4, unstable steady states separate collapse and spreading regimes requiring matched-asymptotic description.

Conclusion: The cooperative reproduction parameter k fundamentally alters population dynamics, producing phase transitions between stable, unstable, and marginal regimes, with self-similar collapse or diffusive spreading depending on parameters.

Abstract: We extend the ``Brownian bees'' model of Berestycki et al. (2021, 2022) to cooperative reproduction, $kA\to(k{+}1)A$, of a population of $N$ symmetric random walkers with removal, at each birth event, of the particle farthest from the origin. Working in the limit $N\to\infty$, we formulate a hydrodynamic free-boundary problem for this model. Using this formalism, we determine steady state population densities for all~$k$ and prove their linear stability for $k\le 2$ and instability for $k\ge 4$. In the marginal case $k=3$, there is a whole continuous family of steady states at a single, critical ratio of the reproduction and diffusion rates. Above criticality the population undergoes an asymptotically self-similar finite-time collapse to the origin. Below the criticality the population spreads diffusively, but the reproduction remains quantitatively relevant. For $k\ge 4$, the unstable steady state separates regimes of a finite-time collapse and a diffusive spreading. Here the collapse dynamics is asymptotically self-similar, and the population density exhibits a scale separation requiring a matched-asymptotic description. Our analytical predictions are confirmed by numerical solutions of the hydrodynamic free-boundary problem and by Monte Carlo simulations of the original microscopic model.
