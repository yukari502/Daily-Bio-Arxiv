### [1] [From Genomes to Algorithms: Neural Network Applications for Palimpsest Detection in Medieval Manuscripts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06889)
*James B. Harr,Madelin E. Blong,Tessa Gadomski,Kelly A. Meiklejohn,William E. Gundling*

Main category: q-bio.GN

TL;DR: This study applies non-destructive DNA sampling and machine learning (logistic regression, neural networks) to analyze a 14th-century manuscript, finding that palimpsest preparation does not significantly degrade DNA and that computational methods can help identify reused parchment, though dataset imbalance limits recall.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To evaluate whether chemical washing during palimpsest preparation compromises DNA integrity, and to explore whether computational methods can aid in identifying reused parchment in historical manuscripts.

Method: Non-destructive sampling of mitochondrial genomes from single-use and palimpsested folios of Ms. Codex 1629, followed by DNA sequencing and machine learning classifiers (logistic regression and neural networks) to distinguish parchment types.

Result: Both single-use and palimpsested parchments retained sufficient mtGenomes with no significant differences in coverage/depth. ML models achieved high precision but reduced recall for the minority palimpsest class due to dataset imbalance.

Conclusion: Integrating molecular biology and neural networks offers new approaches for palimpsest detection, highlighting the evolving role of data science in biocodicology, though more samples and testing are needed.

Abstract: Biocodicology, the study of biological information preserved in manuscripts, offers new opportunities to examine parchment as both a textual and biological artefact. This study applies non-destructive sampling to isolate and sequence mitochondrial genomes (mtGenomes) from a 14th-century manuscript, Ms. Codex 1629, which contains both single-use and palimpsested folios. We sought to evaluate whether palimpsest preparation, including chemical washing, compromised DNA integrity and whether computational methods could aid in identifying reused parchment. DNA sequencing revealed that both single-use and palimpsested parchments retained sufficient mtGenomes for analysis, with no significant differences in genome coverage or depth. To assess the potential of computational biology in manuscript studies, we implemented machine learning classifiers, including logistic regression and neural networks, to distinguish palimpsests from single-use folios. Models achieved high precision but exhibited reduced recall for the minority palimpsest class, reflecting dataset imbalance. While additional ancient mtGenome samples from palimpsest are required and further testing is needed, this study demonstrates how integrating molecular biology and neural networks highlights new approaches for palimpsest detection and underscores the evolving role of data science in biocodicology.

### [2] [Nullclines, Subnullclines and the Asymptotic and Transient Attractors in Eco-Evolutionary Dynamics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07372)
*Krzysztof Argasinski,Manjyot Singh Bedi,Mark Broom*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper analyzes the eco-evolutionary dynamics of the Hawk-Dove game with demographic payoffs, revealing heteroclinic orbits, subnullclines as attractors and barriers, and complex cycling under seasonal environmental perturbations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how mortality (cost, opponent-dependent) and fertility (reward, density-dependent via juvenile recruitment) jointly shape eco-evolutionary dynamics in the Hawk-Dove game, and to extend the framework to include environmental seasonality.

Method: Analysis of frequency-density nullcline intersections, identification of heteroclinic orbits and subnullclines, and extension to periodic background mortality (seasonality).

Result: Stable/unstable equilibria connected by heteroclinic orbits; subnullclines discovered as manifolds that attract trajectories and act as resilience/resistance barriers under seasonal forcing, leading to complex cycles.

Conclusion: The work completes and extends prior eco-evolutionary game analyses by formally linking demographic payoff structures to dynamical phenomena such as heteroclinic orbits and perturbation thresholds under seasonality.

Abstract: In the demographic framework, mortality payoff function describes the cost of an interaction and fertility payoff function describes its reward. So while mortality cost depends on opponent's strategy, fertility reward can be affected by the density-dependent juvenile recruitment survival. This motivates an analysis of the eco-evolutionary dynamics of the classical Hawk-Dove game. It is shown that the stable and unstable equilibria (determined by the intersections of frequency and density nullclines) are connected by heteroclinic orbits, which attract nearby trajectories. The resulting bundle of trajectories leads to the discovery of the so-called subnullcines (manifolds placed between frequency and density nullcline) before they converge to the stable rest point. The initial isolated system is then extended by adding environmental seasonality (periodic background mortality), which acts as an external factor. This leads to complex cycling behavior and the subnullclines act as barriers to the propagation of the perturbation (resilience/resistance threshold). Thus, in a way, this paper completes, yet extends, previous works on the eco-evolutionary dynamics of games with demographic payoffs.

### [8] [Fixed point compositionality via low-rank gluing rules in inhibition-dominated threshold-linear networks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07336)
*Juliana Londono Alvarez*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper formally characterizes how structural modularity (via "low-rank gluings") supports functional compositionality in inhibition-dominated threshold-linear networks, proving that global fixed points are constrained combinations of local module fixed points, and demonstrating applications for engineering compositional dynamics with predictable attractors.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To provide a rigorous mathematical characterization of the relationship between network modularity and functional compositionality in nonlinear networks, which has been lacking despite its observed importance in biological and artificial systems.

Method: Introduces low-rank gluings (and rank-1 gluings) as a modular network assembly framework, where subnetworks with arbitrary internal connectivity are coupled via low-rank connections. Provides theoretical proofs for fixed point decomposition rules and extends results to graph-based networks (gCTLNs).

Result: Proves that global fixed points of low-rank gluings are constrained combinations of local fixed points; provides complete characterization for rank-1 gluings; extends decomposition rules from CTLNs to gCTLNs; demonstrates engineering of networks with combinatorially large predictable attractors including fixed points and limit cycles.

Conclusion: Structural modularity via low-rank gluing provides a mathematically tractable framework for understanding and engineering compositional dynamics in nonlinear networks, with broad implications for neuroscience and artificial intelligence.

Abstract: Brains routinely generate highly flexible and complex behaviors on a relatively stable structure and limited resources. A key mechanism underlying this ability is compositionality, which allows the brain to efficiently decompose complex tasks into simpler, reusable primitives. While network modularity has often been linked to compositionality in biological and artificial networks, a rigorous mathematical characterization of this relationship in nonlinear networks is still lacking. In this work, we formally investigate how structural modularity supports functional compositionality in inhibition-dominated threshold-linear networks (TLNs). We introduce a novel class of modular network assembly called low-rank gluings, where component subnetworks with arbitrary internal connectivity are connected via specific low-rank couplings. We prove that the global fixed points of these networks are constrained to be combinations of the local fixed points of their constituent modules. For a more structured subclass, called rank-1 gluings, we provide a complete characterization that determines which combinations of local fixed points yield global ones. We apply these results to graph-based networks, extending fixed point decomposition rules from combinatorial threshold-linear networks (CTLNs) to the more flexible family of generalized CTLNs (gCTLNs), thereby proving that these structural rules are more robust than initially posited. Finally, we demonstrate that these gluing rules provide a mathematically tractable recipe for engineering compositional dynamics, enabling the construction of networks with a combinatorially large repertoire of predictable attractors that can be understood from simpler component motifs, ranging from compositions of fixed points to compositional limit cycles.
