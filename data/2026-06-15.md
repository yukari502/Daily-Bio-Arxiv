### [1] [From simple interactions to complex biology: a hypergraph percolation perspective](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13738)
*Arturo Tozzi*

Main category: q-bio.OT

TL;DR: This paper uses hypergraph percolation simulations to show that higher-order interactions can drive rapid transitions from fragmented local assemblies to large-scale integrated biological organization, suggesting that the origin of life may have involved such abrupt organizational shifts.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how large-scale biological connectivity emerges from simple, locally interacting elements, and whether this transition can be explained by higher-order interaction dynamics rather than gradual molecular evolution alone.

Method: Computational simulations of hypergraph percolation where elementary units are nodes and collective interactions form hyperedges of variable sizes, with increasing hyperedge density to characterize component growth, fragmentation, interaction overlap, participation, and structural redundancy.

Result: Critical transition regions were observed where sparse local assemblies rapidly reorganized into extensive connected structures, characterized by abrupt expansion of the largest connected component, consolidation of previously disconnected clusters, and increasing overlap among higher-order interactions.

Conclusion: Alongside progressive molecular evolution, the origin of life and emergence of biological organization may have involved rapid organizational transitions driven by higher-order connectivity and interaction architecture.

Abstract: The emergence of biological complexity can be viewed as a transition from fragmented local interactions to extensive integrated organization, raising the question of how large-scale connectivity emerges from simple interacting elements. We investigated whether this transition can be understood as a consequence of hypergraph percolation driven by higher-order interactions. We performed computational simulations where elementary units constituted nodes and collective interactions formed hyperedges of variable size. Increasing hyperedge density enabled the characterization of connected-component growth, fragmentation, interaction overlap, participation and structural redundancy. Critical transition regions were identified as sparse local assemblies rapidly reorganized into extensive connected structures. These transitions were characterized by abrupt expansion of the largest connected component, progressive consolidation of previously disconnected clusters and increasing overlap among higher-order interactions. Connectivity growth was accompanied by the accumulation of alternative pathways and nested interaction patterns, pointing towards large-scale reorganization over a narrow range of interaction densities. Our findings suggest that, alongside the progressive evolution of molecular complexity, the origin of life and the emergence of biological organization may have involved rapid organizational transitions driven by higher-order connectivity and interaction architecture.

### [2] [CisTransCell: Single-Cell Perturbation Prediction via Gene Function, Regulatory Control, and Cellular Context](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13713)
*Wei Zhang,Xun Jiang,Yuesi Xi,Ming Tang*

Main category: q-bio.GN

TL;DR: CisTransCell is a multi-modal framework that integrates regulatory-sequence and coding-sequence priors with cellular expression state to predict transcriptional responses to genetic perturbations, achieving strong zero-shot prediction performance.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Predicting cellular transcriptional responses to unseen genetic perturbations (zero-shot setting) is difficult because perturbation effects depend on complex biological interactions beyond expression state alone, including gene product functions, cis-regulatory elements, and active regulatory programs.

Method: CisTransCell augments each gene with two complementary priors: (1) a regulatory-sequence prior capturing how the gene is controlled, and (2) a coding-sequence prior capturing what the gene product does. These are integrated with cellular expression state to model perturbation response as a cascade from gene function to regulatory control to downstream transcriptional change.

Result: CisTransCell achieves strong performance in zero-shot perturbation prediction on benchmark single-cell perturbation datasets.

Conclusion: Incorporating both regulatory and coding sequence priors improves generalization to unseen perturbations, better capturing the biological complexity of transcriptional responses.

Abstract: Predicting cellular transcriptional responses to genetic perturbations is a central problem in single-cell biology, especially in the zero-shot setting where the perturbed gene or gene combination is unseen during training. A major difficulty is that perturbation effects are not determined by expression state alone: they depend on how the perturbed gene product influences other genes and proteins, how those downstream factors act on cis-regulatory elements, and which regulatory programs are active in the current cell state. To better capture this biological complexity, we propose CisTransCell, a cell-conditioned multi-modal framework for single-cell perturbation prediction that augments each gene with two complementary priors: a regulatory-sequence prior that captures how the gene is controlled, and a coding-sequence prior that captures what the gene product does. By integrating these priors with cellular expression state, CisTransCell models perturbation response as a cascade from gene function to regulatory control to downstream transcriptional change. Experiments on benchmark single-cell perturbation datasets show that CisTransCell achieves strong performance in zero-shot perturbation prediction.

### [3] [Decoding Semantic Categories from Picture-Naming EEG](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14614)
*Wei Hu,Binbin Xu*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This study demonstrates that semantic-category information can be decoded from high-density EEG during overt picture naming, achieving 78.1% balanced accuracy when combining early and naming-related temporal windows, supporting the use of neural decoding for investigating lexical-semantic processing.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To investigate whether semantic-category information is recoverable from high-density EEG signals during overt picture naming, which requires understanding the neural basis of lexical-semantic processing in spoken language production.

Method: 16 French speakers performed picture naming with line drawings. Picture labels were embedded using multilingual text-embedding model into 9 semantic categories. EEG was encoded via a pre-trained single-channel encoder over early post-stimulus, naming-related, and combined windows, with 9-class decoding analysis.

Result: Above-chance semantic-category discrimination in all temporal windows. Balanced accuracy: 0.562 (early), 0.610 (naming-related), 0.781 (combined). Maximum Macro-F1: 0.784. Consistent gains across categories with spatially distributed sensor-level decoding.

Conclusion: Semantic-category structure is reflected in EEG during overt picture naming; early and naming-related windows provide complementary information; modern neural decoding methods are useful tools for studying lexical-semantic processing in spoken language production.

Abstract: Picture naming requires the transformation of visual object information into a spoken lexical response through perceptual, semantic, lexical, and articulatory processes. This study asked whether semantic-category information is recoverable from high-density EEG during overt picture naming. Sixteen native French-speaking participants performed a picture-naming task using line drawings. Picture labels were embedded with a multilingual text-embedding model and organized into nine interpretable semantic categories, providing a data-driven semantic target space for neural decoding. EEG activity was represented channel-wise using a pre-trained single-channel EEG encoder over an early post-stimulus window, a later naming-related window, and their combination. Nine-class decoding showed above-chance semantic-category discrimination in all temporal representations. Balanced accuracy increased from 0.562 in the early window to 0.610 in the naming-related window, and reached 0.781 when both windows were combined, with a maximum Macro-F1 of 0.784. Class-level F1 scores showed consistent gains across semantic categories, and sensor-level decoding maps indicated spatially distributed category information. These findings suggest that semantic-category structure is reflected in EEG activity during overt picture naming and that early and naming-related temporal windows provide complementary information. The results support the use of modern neural decoding methods as tools for investigating lexical-semantic processing in spoken language production.

### [4] [Prospective Coding and Path Integration Emerge as Equilibrium Solutions of Self-Organizing Neural Networks with Firing-Rate Adaptation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14649)
*Facundo Emina,Emilio Kropff*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper shows how continuous attractor neural networks (CANN) can self-organize through Hebbian plasticity, adaptation, and inhibition, naturally giving rise to anticipatory dynamics and path integration without requiring pre-wired recurrent connectivity.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To uncover the biological learning mechanisms behind the structured connectivity of CANNs, which are traditionally pre-wired rather than learned.

Method: The authors develop a theoretical framework combining Hebbian plasticity, firing-rate adaptation, and global inhibition to demonstrate self-organization of CANN connectivity and its computational properties.

Result: Translationally invariant inputs yield Gaussian feedforward weights; anticipatory shifts emerge spontaneously in feedforward architectures and amplify across layers; recurrent interactions enable self-sustaining moving bumps; speed-modulated baseline currents enable precise unidirectional path integration.

Conclusion: Prospective coding and path integration are naturally co-emergent properties of a self-organizing competitive network, not manually engineered features.

Abstract: Continuous Attractor Neural Networks (CANNs) traditionally rely on pre-wired recurrent connectivity to model spatial representations, path integration, and anticipatory dynamics. However, the biological mechanisms through which this structured connectivity emerges via learning remain relatively unexplored. This work presents a theoretical framework revealing how continuous attractor connectivity and its computational properties self-organize through Hebbian plasticity, firing-rate adaptation, and global inhibition. We show that translationally invariant inputs naturally drive the emergence of stable, Gaussian-profiled feedforward weights. Crucially, anticipatory dynamics arise spontaneously within these feedforward architectures, shifting the activity bump forward without requiring recurrent excitatory collaterals. This predictive shift can be linearly amplified across multilayer networks, consistent with anticipatory activity observed in the superficial layers of the entorhinal cortex. Furthermore, introducing recurrent interactions allows the network to learn connections capable of self-sustaining a moving bump of activity. Finally, by modulating the network with an external, time-varying baseline current that encodes speed, the system adjusts its intrinsic velocity to function as a precise unidirectional path integrator. Ultimately, this study suggests that prospective coding and path integration are not manually engineered features, but rather naturally co-emergent properties of a single self-organizing competitive network.

### [6] [Mean First Passage Time for Persistent Random Walks in Annular Search Domains](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13890)
*Fatemeh Saghafifar,Daniel Coombs*

Main category: q-bio.CB

TL;DR: This paper studies how long it takes a random walker (modeling immune cells) to find a small target at the center of a 2D annulus with a reflecting outer boundary, analyzing how directional persistence and chemotactic bias affect search efficiency using analytical and numerical methods.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Quantify how long immune cells (natural killer cells) take to reach a target cancer cell, and understand how search efficiency depends on directional persistence and chemotactic bias.

Method: Models cell motion as a velocity-jump process (correlated random walk with von Mises turning kernel; extends to biased correlated random walk via phase-shifted kernel). Combines closed-form benchmarks, Fourier-mode reductions, fast-turning perturbation expansion, and numerical methods (semi-Lagrangian solver, stationary discretisation, event-driven Monte Carlo).

Result: Provides analytical corrections to the diffusion-limit mean first-passage time, supported by numerical cross-validation, establishing a framework that links persistent/biased motion to target-search times in confined 2D domains.

Conclusion: The framework enables relating persistent and biased immune-cell motion to target-search times in confined two-dimensional domains.

Abstract: We study the mean first-passage time of a random walker to a small absorbing target at the center of a two-dimensional annulus with a specularly reflecting outer boundary. The problem is motivated by natural killer cell migration toward a target cancer cell, where the goal is to quantify how long it takes immune cells to reach the target and how search efficiency depends on directional persistence and chemotactic bias. Cell motion is modeled as a velocity-jump process. We first consider a correlated random walk with a von Mises turning kernel, with a concentration parameter controlling directional persistence. We then extend the model to a biased correlated random walk using a phase-shifted turning kernel that represents preferential motion, for example following a concentration gradient. Our analysis combines closed-form benchmarks for simple and biased random walks, Fourier-mode reductions of the transport equations for the correlated and biased correlated models, and a fast-turning perturbation expansion that gives an analytical correction to the diffusion-limit mean first-passage time for the random walker. Our analytical results are supported by numerical methods that include a semi-Lagrangian solver in radial and angular coordinates, a stationary discretisation designed to handle biased transport, and an event-driven Monte Carlo simulator for cross-validation. Together, our results provide a framework relating persistent and biased immune-cell motion to target-search times in confined two-dimensional domains.

### [7] [An analytical framework to unify ecological and engineering resilience near critical transitions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13921)
*Tristan Gamot,Tom J. M. Van Dooren*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper develops a theoretical framework using normal form theory to derive explicit scaling laws for ecological and engineering resilience metrics near bifurcation-induced tipping points, showing that the rates at which these metrics decrease depend on bifurcation type and that resiliences are intrinsically linked near local bifurcations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand how ecological and engineering resilience jointly change near critical transitions (tipping points), as characterizing these changes is central to anticipating and detecting such shifts in complex systems.

Method: The authors use normal form theory to derive explicit scaling laws for resilience metrics as functions of distance to the bifurcation point in parameter space, and validate these predictions on three representative models covering all common local bifurcations in one-dimensional systems.

Result: The derived scaling laws extend to general models up to a scaling factor; the rates at which resilience metrics decrease depend on both the type of bifurcation and the metric considered; resiliences are intrinsically linked sufficiently close to a local bifurcation.

Conclusion: The framework provides more rigorous scaling predictions (replacing previous heuristic arguments) and shows that relative behavior of metrics is more important than absolute values for tipping detection.

Abstract: The capacity of dynamical systems to resist and recover from perturbations, broadly referred to as resilience, is commonly expressed by two complementary quantities: ecological and engineering resilience. As many complex systems exhibit critical transitions, or tipping points, understanding how these resiliences jointly change nearby them is central to characterising and anticipating such shifts. Here, we develop a theoretical framework that clarifies this for bifurcation-induced tipping, i.e., critical transitions triggered by the crossing of a local bifurcation. Using normal form theory, we derive explicit scaling laws for commonly used resilience metrics as functions of the distance to the bifurcation point in parameter space, and show that these extend to general models up to a scaling factor. They are particularly relevant for detecting tipping, where the relative behaviour of metrics matters more than their absolute values. The rates at which metrics decrease as the bifurcation is approached depend on both the type of bifurcation and the metric considered. Furthermore, our results show that, sufficiently close to a local bifurcation, resiliences are intrinsically linked. Our predictions, which replace previously proposed scalings based on heuristic arguments, are validated for three representative models covering all commonly encountered local bifurcations in one-dimensional systems.

</details>
