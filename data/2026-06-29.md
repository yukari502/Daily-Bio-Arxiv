### [1] [Towards coevolution-aware ancestral sequence reconstruction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27942)
*Alya Zeinaty,Leonardo di Bari,Saverio Rossi,Pierre Barrat-Charlaix,Francesco Zamponi,Martin Weigt*

Main category: q-bio.BM

TL;DR: Introduces a coevolution-aware ancestral sequence reconstruction (ASR) framework that combines phylogenetic inference with Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA) to account for epistatic constraints, improving reconstruction accuracy under realistic protein evolution.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Standard ASR methods assume site independence, ignoring epistatic constraints that shape protein structure, stability, and function. This leads to over-concentrated or implausible ancestral reconstructions.

Method: Combines standard phylogenetic inference with Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA) to enforce residue-residue constraints from extant protein families while preserving site-wise ancestral uncertainty. Benchmarked using a forward-evolution framework based on a DCA evolutionary sampler.

Result: Applied to beta-lactamases and DNA-binding domains; improves reconstruction accuracy under epistatic constraints and yields ensembles that are both phylogenetically consistent and statistically compatible with natural protein families.

Conclusion: Bridges the gap between single-sequence MAP reconstruction and unconstrained posterior sampling, offering a practical route to ancestors that better reflect the coupled nature of protein evolution.

Abstract: Ancestral sequence reconstruction (ASR) is a powerful approach for studying molecular evolution and the emergence of protein function. Yet most ASR methods assume that sites evolve independently, neglecting the epistatic constraints that shape protein structure, stability, and function. This simplification affects both ancestral inference and its evaluation: maximum-a-posteriori reconstructions may over-concentrate probability into a single over-idealized sequence, whereas independent posterior sampling can generate implausible or poorly functional ancestors. Here, we introduce a coevolution-aware ASR framework that combines standard phylogenetic inference with Direct Coupling Analysis (DCA), thereby preserving site-wise ancestral uncertainty while enforcing residue-residue constraints learned from extant protein families. To benchmark the method, we develop a controlled forward-evolution framework based on a DCA evolutionary sampler, allowing reconstructed ancestors to be compared with known ground-truth sequences generated under realistic epistatic constraints. Applied to beta-lactamases and DNA-binding domains, the approach improves reconstruction when ancestral states are epistatically constrained, and yields ensembles of candidate ancestors that are both phylogenetically consistent and statistically compatible with natural protein families. This framework bridges the gap between single-sequence MAP reconstruction and unconstrained posterior sampling, providing a practical route toward ancestral reconstructions that better reflect the coupled nature of protein evolution.

TL;DR: This paper uses generative sequence landscapes to benchmark evolutionary intermediate reconstruction from endpoint sequences, finding that maximum-likelihood prediction can be inaccurate, conditional sampling better captures plausible histories, and predictability depends on landscape topology—with constrained regions preserving path information and mutable regions erasing it—while also showing that sequence divergence alone is insufficient for temporal placement.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To determine what can and cannot be inferred about evolutionary intermediates from endpoint sequences, given that the true sequence of steps producing them is rarely recoverable from extant data alone.

Method: Using generative sequence landscapes as controlled models of protein-family evolution, the authors benchmark data-driven reconstruction against ground-truth simulated trajectories, comparing maximum-likelihood point predictions versus conditional sampling approaches.

Result: Maximum-likelihood intermediates can be residue-wise accurate but statistically atypical; conditional sampling better captures plausible evolutionary histories. Predictability is limited by landscape topology—constrained low-mutability regions preserve path information while permissive high-mutability regions open alternative routes. Sequence divergence alone is insufficient; incorporating endpoint mutability better estimates elapsed evolutionary time.

Conclusion: Intermediate reconstruction should be treated as a calibrated probabilistic problem—rather than seeking a single "true" sequence, models should identify when endpoints contain evolutionary information and return realistic ensembles of plausible intermediates.

Abstract: Evolutionary intermediates connect observed proteins, but the sequence of steps that produced them is rarely recoverable from extant data alone. Here we ask what can, and cannot, be inferred about such intermediates from the endpoints. Using generative sequence landscapes as controlled models of protein-family evolution, we benchmark data-driven reconstruction against ground-truth simulated trajectories. We find that the best point prediction is not necessarily the most faithful evolutionary reconstruction: maximum-likelihood intermediates can be residue-wise accurate yet statistically atypical, whereas conditional sampling better captures the ensemble of plausible histories. Predictability is limited by the topology of the landscape. Constrained, low-mutability regions preserve information about the path, while permissive high-mutability regions open many alternative routes and erase path-specific memory. We also show that sequence divergence alone is an insufficient measure of elapsed evolutionary time; incorporating endpoint mutability provides a more reliable way to place intermediates in the landscape. These results recast intermediate reconstruction as a calibrated probabilistic problem. Rather than seeking a single "true" sequence, data-driven models should identify when endpoints contain evolutionary information, and return realistic ensembles.
