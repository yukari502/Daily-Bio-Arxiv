### [1] [Governable Individuals: An Identity Layer for Embodied Agents That Keep Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05463)
*Xue Qin,Simin Luan,Cong Yang,Zhijun Li*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: The paper proposes "governable individuals" - persistent AI agents that can continuously learn and evolve, but whose authority and capabilities can only expand through cryptographically signed lifecycle transitions recorded in a public identity commitment. The key insight is that governance must be architectural rather than behavioral for agents that rewrite themselves.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Existing governance approaches (agent IDs, activity logs, guardrails) fail for persistent, self-modifying agents because these agents keep rewriting themselves, making traditional governance mechanisms ineffective. The paper aims to solve how to govern an agent that continuously evolves its own competence. 

Method: The authors propose an abstraction called a "governable individual" with a runtime mechanism where: (1) the agent's competence can change freely, but (2) its authority, memory schema, embodiment rights, and capability roster can only expand through signed lifecycle transitions that update a public identity commitment. This is an architectural, not behavioral, solution.

Result: Testing showed that neither learned judgment nor behavioral testing alone was sufficient for governance - the load-bearing layer must be architectural. The approach works where softer governance mechanisms fail for self-modifying agents.

Conclusion: The paper presents the governable individual abstraction and its runtime mechanism, acknowledging open problems remain. The core finding is that governance of persistent, self-modifying AI agents requires architectural constraints rather than behavioral oversight.

Abstract: Embodied artificial intelligence is moving from deployable models to persistent agents that learn in the field, acquire skills and migrate across bodies. Governing such a system means governing an individual, not a model, and existing proposals (agent identifiers, activity logs, guardrails) do not survive an agent that keeps rewriting itself. We propose the governable individual: an agent whose competence may change without bound, but whose authority, memory schema, embodiment rights and capability roster can widen only through signed lifecycle transitions that update a public identity commitment. In our tests, neither learned judgement nor behavioural testing was sufficient to carry this on its own; the load-bearing layer must be architectural. We describe the abstraction, a runtime mechanism that realizes it, and the open problems in between.

### [2] [On the Increased and Decreased Connectivity of the Demented Human Brain](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05654)
*Daniel Hegedus,Marton Barnabas Mora,Balint Varga,Vince Grolmusz*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This study analyzes the OASIS-3 brain imaging dataset to investigate gray matter structural connectivity changes in aging and dementia. Results show decreased connectivity in hippocampus and temporal lobe structures, but increased connectivity in precuneus, cuneus, and insula regions in dementia.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: With advances in cerebral imaging providing abundant data on brain aging and dementia, there is a need to identify specific gray matter regions where structural connectivity changes occur, to better understand the neural underpinnings of dementia and aging.

Method: The study applies the OASIS-3 dataset and analyzes structural connectivity patterns in human gray matter, comparing connectivity differences between healthy aging and dementia states.

Result: Finer structures of the hippocampus and temporal lobe show decreased connectivity in dementia. Conversely, the precuneus, cuneus, and finer structures in the insula exhibit increased connectivity in dementia compared to healthy states.

Conclusion: Dementia involves both decreased connectivity (hippocampus, temporal lobe) and increased connectivity (precuneus, cuneus, insula) in different gray matter regions, revealing a more complex pattern of structural brain changes than simple degeneration.

Abstract: With the enormous advances in cerebral imaging techniques, a large amount of data is available for studying the aging and demented brain. In this contribution, we apply the OASIS-3 dataset for identifying small areas of the human gray matter, which have higher- or lower structural connectivity in dementia and aging. As anticipated, we found that finer structures of the hippocampus and the temporal lobe show decreased connectivity in dementia. More surprisingly, the precuneus, the cuneus, and finer structures in the insula show higher connectivity in dementia than in the healthy state.

<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>

Method: A Maximum Entropy model of group personalities is constructed, then decimated to a single "collective personality" vector. Personality scores are augmented with intelligence and emotional quotients to reveal collective intelligence. Simple matrix analyses are performed on the collective personality structure.

Abstract: We propose a mathematical framework for organizational psychology based on a Maximum Entropy model of a group's personalities. With simple matrix analyses of the collective personality, one can understand: that weak interpersonal coupling can strongly affect group character; that malleable rather than stubborn personalities control the group's collective personality; that one can mathematically solve for optimal top-down directives to achieve certain group personalities; and that groups can have a personality disorder even if the individuals composing the groups are neurotypical. We hope that this framework provides a useful starting point for future mathematical analyses in organizational psychology related to innate character rather than opinion dynamics or decision making, and note that the analysis can be applied to much more complex Maximum Entropy models than the one proposed here if empirical evidence suggests that the Gaussian model proposed here is overly simplistic.

<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>

Method: Using weekly case counts (1993-2012, 246,524 cases) across all 81 cantons, the authors reconstructed a canton-to-canton spread map based on the ~3-week dengue generation interval, removed shared seasonal/climatic signals to isolate direction-specific spread, and summarized results using receiving/source cantons, amplification factor, and directionality index over 5-year windows.

Local outbreaks are amplified ~3-4x across the network despite stable overall transmission. Abstract: \textbf{Background:} In Costa Rica, dengue is reported and controlled at the canton level, and outbreaks in one canton are often followed by outbreaks in others.   \noindent \textbf{Methods:} From weekly case counts for all 81 cantons (1993--2012; \num{246524} cases) we reconstructed a canton-to-canton spread map using the roughly three-week dengue generation interval, removed the shared seasonal and climatic signal so that only direction-specific spread remained, and summarized it by the receiving and source cantons, an amplification factor, and a directionality index, tracked over five-year windows.

  \noindent \textbf{Conclusions:} Routine surveillance alone can map which cantons tend to experience dengue and the pathways through which it appears to spread, providing a potential input for prioritizing surveillance and vector control, particularly when a serotype or the disease itself is newly establishing. Abstract: We extend the standard susceptible-infected-recovered framework to incorporate natural immune boosting during a short-scale outbreak.
