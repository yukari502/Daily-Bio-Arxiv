### [1] [Causal Discovery of Radiation Response Mechanisms in Human Cells](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13994)
*Ashka Shah,Rick Stevens*

Main category: q-bio.GN

TL;DR: This paper applies causal discovery to model gene regulatory networks from RNA-sequencing data, revealing directed relationships between radiation dose rate perturbations and gene expression that outperform correlation-based methods in identifying known radiation response pathways.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Standard bioinformatics tools for differential expression analysis are univariate, linear, and rely on predefined pathway annotations, limiting their ability to capture nonlinear and multivariate gene interactions underlying disease and treatment response.

Method: The authors apply causal discovery to jointly model radiation perturbations and gene expression data, learning directed gene networks that capture causal regulatory relationships beyond correlation.

Result: Inferred causal graphs are significantly enriched for known radiation response pathways, reveal structured network features (high in-degree housekeeping genes, high out-degree transcription factors), and suggest hierarchical organization of stress response and cell death pathways.

Conclusion: Causal discovery shows strong potential for healthcare applications, including understanding response mechanisms, identifying regulatory targets, and improving interpretation of complex genomic data.

Abstract: Next-generation sequencing technologies, including RNA-sequencing, provide genome-wide measurements of gene expression and enable broad explorations of biomarkers and mechanisms underlying disease and treatment response. Bioinformatics tools for processing this data, such as differential expression analysis, are largely univariate, linear, and rely on predefined pathway knowledge annotations, which limits their ability to capture nonlinear and multivariate gene interactions. This paper explores the application of causal discovery to characterizing transcriptional responses to radiation as a function of dose rate in human cells. By jointly modeling radiation perturbations and gene expression, we learn directed gene networks that capture important regulatory relationships beyond correlation and exhibit significant enrichment of known radiation response pathways compared to baseline approaches. We find that inferred causal graphs reveal structured network features such as high in-degree housekeeping genes and high out-degree transcription factors. Further analysis suggests a hierarchical organization of stress response pathways and triggered cell death pathways. This work highlights the potential of causal discovery in healthcare settings with applications to understanding response mechanisms, identifying regulatory targets, and improving interpretation of complex genomic data.

### [2] [Screening of Biosecurity Features in Metagenomic Data with Evo 2 Probes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14070)
*Jeremy Guntoro,Alexander Dack,Dylan Danno,Michaela Jančovičová,Križan Jurinović,Vanessa Smilansky*

Main category: q-bio.GN

TL;DR: This paper shows that lightweight probes trained on frozen Evo 2 representations can detect antimicrobial resistance (ROC-AUC 0.888–0.977) and bacterial virulence (0.833) in metagenomic sequences without fine-tuning the foundation model, offering a fast first-pass biosecurity screening method.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Genomic foundation models (e.g., Evo 2) are powerful but their utility for biosecurity screening remains unexplored. The authors investigate whether biosecurity-relevant signals are linearly accessible in frozen model representations, avoiding expensive fine-tuning.

Method: Linear and single-head attention probes trained on frozen Evo 2 layer-26 activations (no fine-tuning). Tested on held-out metagenomic data for AMR and virulence detection. Also evaluated on simulated short reads and against Evo 1.5-generated sequences (SynGenome). Complementary sparse-autoencoder analysis.

Result: Linear probe achieves region-level ROC-AUC 0.888 (mean-pool) for AMR, rising to 0.977 with attention probe. AMR drug-class subcategories resolved. Virulence detection weaker (0.833). Read-level AMR detection ROC-AUC 0.898 without retraining. Prompt labels from Evo 1.5 sequences weakly recoverable. Sparse autoencoders less consistent than probes.

Conclusion: Lightweight embedding-based probes are a fast, inexpensive first-pass detection layer for metagenomic biosurveillance, with clear strengths and current limits.

Abstract: Genomic foundation models such as Evo 2 learn rich sequence representations, but their value for biosecurity screening is largely unexplored. We ask how much biosecurity-relevant signal is linearly accessible in these representations by training minimal linear and attention probes on frozen Evo 2 layer-26 activations, without fine-tuning the underlying model. Across held-out metagenomic test sets, the probes detect antimicrobial resistance (AMR) with strong discrimination: a linear probe reaches a region-level ROC-AUC of 0.888 (mean-pool), rising to 0.977 with a single-head attention probe. The probes resolve finer-grained AMR drug-class subcategories and separate them from unrelated functional genes, providing additional evidence that the learned signal is not explained solely by generic functional-gene status. Bacterial virulence is also decodable, though more weakly (region-level ROC-AUC 0.833). The AMR probe retains comparable ranking performance on simulated short reads without retraining, enabling evaluation before assembly in settings where assembly is computationally costly or unreliable. It achieves a read-level ROC-AUC of 0.898 (mean-pool), comparable to the mean-pooled full-region result. Within SynGenome, AMR-associated prompt labels are only weakly recoverable from Evo 1.5-generated sequences; these prompt-derived labels do not establish the function of the generated response sequences. A complementary sparse-autoencoder analysis recovers interpretable resistance-associated features but proves less consistent than the supervised probes. Together, these results position lightweight embedding-based probes as a fast, inexpensive first-pass detection layer for metagenomic biosurveillance and map both strengths and current limits of the approach. This work was conducted as part of the AIxBio Hackathon 2026 hosted by BlueDot Impact, Apart Research, and Cambridge Biosecurity Hub.

### [3] [Classifying daily activities needs posture, reconstructing them needs motion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13216)
*Arefeh Farahmandi,Gunnar Blohm*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: The study compares three movement analysis strategies (TMPs, Legendre coefficients, autoencoders) for classifying 16 daily activities. Legendre coefficients and TMPs yield highest classification accuracy, yet only TMPs can generate perceptually natural movement reconstructions. The authors identify general body posture as the most discriminative feature for classification, revealing a dissociation: static posture suffices for recognition, but temporal dynamics are essential for movement generation.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To systematically compare different strategies of movement analysis and identify what information in visual stimuli allows humans to rapidly classify movements, addressing the gap that no framework has systematically compared such approaches before.

Method: Used videos of 16 daily activities from MoVi dataset, comparing Temporal Movement Primitives (TMPs), Legendre polynomial coefficients, and Autoencoder latent embeddings for movement classification and reconstruction.

Result: Legendre coefficients and TMPs achieved highest classifier accuracy; general posture is the most discriminative feature; 9 critical joints identified as most predictive. Good classification accuracy did not predict good movement generation—Legendre reconstructions appeared frozen while TMPs preserved natural motion.

Conclusion: Static body configuration suffices for classifying what activity is performed, but temporal dynamics are required for reconstructing how movement unfolds. Postural features may enable efficient clinical screening, while dynamic information remains essential for movement generation.

Abstract: Humans recognize movements effortlessly, even from noisy and complex visual input. But what information in the stimulus allows humans to rapidly classify movements? No framework has systematically compared different strategies of movement analysis to address this question. Here, we used videos of 16 daily activities from the MoVi dataset and compared three strategies: Temporal Movement Primitives (TMPs), which decompose movements into weighted sums of temporally smooth basis functions; Legendre polynomial coefficients, which project joint-coordinate trajectories onto an orthogonal polynomial basis; and Autoencoder latent embeddings. Legendre coefficients and TMPs achieved the highest classifier accuracy, followed by autoencoders. We found two discriminative features for movement classification. The most informative is the general posture of the body, the average spatial configuration that distinguishes one activity from another. Additionally, we identified 9 critical joints that are most predictive for movement classification. Interestingly, good classification accuracy did not automatically lead to good movement generation: when we reconstructed movements for each activity, TMPs preserved the temporal dynamics and produced perceptually natural motion, whereas reconstructions from Legendre coefficients retained only the average posture and appeared frozen. These results reveal a dissociation in how movement information is organized: the static configuration of the body suffices to classify what activity is performed, but the temporal dynamics of movement are required to reconstruct how it unfolds. This distinction clarifies which features the visual system may rely upon for rapid action recognition, and suggests that postural features could enable efficient movement screening in clinical applications, while dynamic information remain essential wherever movement generation is the goal.

### [4] [Grounded world models in biological organisms and future embodied AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13560)

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper argues that biological intelligence is organized around grounded world models acquired through interaction, contrasting with current AI's passive, language-centric learning. <details>
  <summary>Details</summary>

Result: Key features missing from current AI include: intrinsic dynamics as foundation for learning, action's centrality in aligning dynamics with the external world, autonomous open-ended learning over passive data assimilation, and early predictive/control mechanisms scaffolding higher cognition (reasoning, planning, imagination, theory of mind, communication).

Abstract: Recent advances in generative and embodied AI have been driven by large-scale predictive learning over multimodal data. However, the resulting systems remain largely based on passive training regimes where linguistic regularities create the scaffold onto which information from other modalities is attached. Conversely, neuroscience and cognitive science suggest that biological intelligence is organized in the opposite way, where grounded world models acquired through interaction with the environment provide the semantic scaffold to which language is attached. Here, we illustrate five examples of neural circuits supporting grounded world modelling, which underlie navigation in physical and conceptual spaces, affordance-based perception and interaction with objects, active perception and exploratory learning, allostatic control and emotion, and the distinction between self- and world-generated outcomes. Finally, we discuss whether and how principles derived from biological systems may inform future embodied AI, including training regimes based on social interaction to construct world models that are not only grounded but also socially shared and aligned with human norms and values.

### [5] [Activity Regeneration from Silent States in Neuronal Networks with Transient Synaptic Memory](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14000)
*Mozhgan Khanjanianpak,Alireza Valiadeh*

Main category: q-bio.NC

TL;DR: This paper shows that transient synaptic memory alone, specifically the Latent Excitatory Recruitment (LER) capacity measured from the residual synaptic state after neuronal silence, can predict whether a network will spontaneously regenerate activity or remain silent, without needing to simulate future dynamics.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To determine whether hidden synaptic states (transient synaptic memory) contain sufficient information to predict the future evolution of neuronal networks after neuronal activity has ceased, addressing the gap in understanding how short-term information might be maintained without persistent firing.

Method: The authors introduce a minimal neuronal network model with finite-lifetime synapses, analyze the synaptic configuration at the first silent state, and identify the Latent Excitatory Recruitment (LER) capacity—quantified as the cumulative number of fresh excitatory neurons—as a predictor of multi-cycle dynamics.

Result: The residual synaptic state at the first silent state determines whether network activity terminates after a single cycle or regenerates. LER capacity serves as a near-perfect predictor of multi-cycle dynamics without requiring further simulation. Distinct dynamical outcomes emerge in an otherwise homogeneous network.

Conclusion: Transient synaptic memory alone is sufficient to generate diverse future dynamics. Short-term memory is encoded not only in ongoing neuronal activity but also in latent synaptic configurations. The snapshot-based framework offers a new perspective for predicting and controlling the future evolution of neuronal networks.

Abstract: Transient synaptic memory has emerged as a potential mechanism for maintaining short-term information even in the absence of persistent neuronal activity. However, it remains unclear whether the hidden synaptic state alone contains sufficient information to predict the future evolution of neuronal networks after activity has ceased. Here, we introduce a minimal neuronal network model with finite-lifetime synapses and investigate the mechanism underlying spontaneous activity regeneration following complete neuronal silence. We show that the residual synaptic configuration at the first silent state already determines whether network activity terminates after a single activation cycle or spontaneously regenerates an additional cycle. By analyzing this synaptic-memory snapshot, we identify the Latent Excitatory Recruitment (LER) capacity, quantified by the cumulative number of fresh excitatory neurons, as a near-perfect predictor of multi-cycle dynamics without continuing the subsequent network simulation. Remarkably, these distinct dynamical outcomes emerge in an otherwise homogeneous neuronal network, demonstrating that transient synaptic memory alone is sufficient to generate diverse future dynamics. Our findings provide a mechanistic explanation for activity regeneration from a residual synaptic state and suggest that short-term memory is encoded not only in ongoing neuronal activity but also in the latent synaptic configuration that preserves the network's capacity to recruit new neuronal assemblies. More broadly, the proposed snapshot-based framework offers a new perspective for predicting and potentially controlling the future evolution of neuronal networks.

### [7] [Predator-dependent replicator dynamics or a predator-prey model with two prey types and frequency dependence](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13281)
*Henrique M. Cruz,Armando G. M. Neves*

Main category: q-bio.PE

TL;DR: This paper reinterprets a three-species competition-predation model (two prey, one predator) as a Lotka-Volterra-type model with frequency-dependent terms, proving stability conditions for equilibria and revealing bifurcation cascades and limit cycles.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To provide an alternative, unifying perspective on a previously proposed three-species population model, deriving clearer conditions for coexistence and equilibrium stability, and uncovering the full dynamical richness including bifurcations and limit cycles.

Method: The authors reformulate the Braga-Wardil model as a Lotka-Volterra-type system with logistic prey growth and frequency-dependent reproduction/capture coefficients. They analyze equilibrium existence conditions and use stability theorems to link two-species equilibrium stability changes with the emergence/disappearance of three-species equilibria.

Result: Conditions for existence of equilibria with all three species are derived. Theorems on stability/instability of two-species equilibria are proved, with stability changes linked to three-species equilibrium bifurcations. A rich cascade of bifurcations appears when varying carrying capacity, including predator extinction, coexistence with one or two prey types, and stable limit cycles involving all species.

Conclusion: The alternative Lotka-Volterra formulation provides clearer insights into the dynamics of the three-species system, revealing complex bifurcation behavior and the conditions under which stable coexistence or cyclic dynamics occur.

Abstract: Braga and Wardil [J. Phys. A: Math. Theor. 55 (2022) 025601] introduced a population model for two prey species that compete among themselves and are preyed upon by a single predator species. They showed the existence of 16 dynamic scenarios and stated sufficient conditions for the stable coexistence of the three species. The model, which can be seen as replicator dynamics with predator-dependent fitnesses for the prey, is based on two pay-off matrices: one for prey reproduction and one for interaction between prey and predators.
  We argue that the model can also be seen as a Lotka-Volterra-type predator-prey model with a single prey species, logistic limitation for prey, and frequency-dependent reproduction and capture coefficients. Using this alternative viewpoint, we obtain conditions for the existence of equilibria with the three types of individuals. We also prove theorems on the stability or instability of equilibria with only two species and relate the stability change of these equilibria to the appearance or disappearance of equilibria with the three species.
  When all parameters, except the one that regulates carrying capacities, are fixed, a rich cascade of bifurcations may appear. Solutions range from predator extinction due to insufficient prey to predators coexisting with one or two prey types. Sometimes stable limit cycles involving all species appear.

</details>
